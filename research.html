My Master's Thesis

My research uses machine learning and deep learning to predict antibiotic resistance in Escherichia coli (E. coli). This project combines large-scale genomic data to understand how genetic variation drives resistance patterns.

Overview

Traditional lab testing is slow and limited to cultivable strains. By applying computational models to genomic data, our goal is to improve prediction speed and accuracy for public health applications.

Problem

Rising antibiotic resistance in E. coli reduces treatment options and increases global health risks. Existing resistance testing is time-consuming, while machine learning enables faster, data-driven insights.

Solution

Our lab built a pangenome dataset of 589 E. coli isolates, covering more than 5.7 million nucleotide positions. This resource captures both shared and unique genetic regions across strains.

Three Genomic Approaches

  • Gene Presence/Absence: Identifies major resistance genes and virulence factors.
  • SNPs: Examines point mutations that alter susceptibility.
  • Alignment-based Features: Captures larger structural variations like insertions or deletions.

Research Questions

  • How well do models trained in one region generalize to others?
  • Does combining multi-country data improve predictive accuracy?
