My Research

Thesis: Predicting E. coli Drug Resistance Using Traditional Machine Learning and Deep Learning

My research centers on building computational pipelines and machine learning models that can predict antibiotic resistance in E. coli. For my thesis, I focus on a single antibiotic Ciprofloxacin (CIP) and use two complementary datasets: gene presence/absence and single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs). The goal is to understand how specific features influence resistance to CIP and develop predictive models that can identify resistant strains based on their genomics.

The Problem

Antibiotic-resistant infections pose a serious global health threat. One of the main causes is drug-resistant E. coli. Typically, testing whether a bacterial strain is resistant takes a lot of time in the lab. With faster genome sequencing and machine learning, we can attempt to predict resistance directly from DNA sequences. The challenge is that an E. coli genome (or shell-genome) contains thousands of genes and mutations ‚Äî not all of which are important for resistance. Finding the right signals in all that data is a hard task, and that‚Äôs where machine learning can help.

Methods

I tackled this challenge by developing a machine learning pipeline that turns raw genomic data into actionable predictions of antibiotic resistance. My approach leverages complementary genomic feature sets and modern ML techniques:

  • Data: I used shell-genome data from 588 E. coli strains, each labeled as Resistant or Susceptible to Ciprofloxacin.
  • Feature Engineering: I extracted two types of genomic features:
    • Gene Presence/Absence Matrix: 6,093 genes indicating presence/absence across strains. These genes originate from the shell genome (shared by some but not all strains). Constant features were removed.
    • SNP Variant Profiles: 166,415 single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) across all strains. SNPs were filtered using minor allele frequency (MAF) and gap-threshold to remove low-quality variants.
  • Model Training: With these feature sets, I trained and tuned classification models to predict resistance. I experimented with algorithms (logistic regression, random forests, SVM) and used rigorous cross-validation to avoid overfitting high-dimensional data. Feature selection and regularization helped focus on the most informative signals.
  • Evaluation & Comparison: I assessed model performance separately for gene-presence features and SNP features to compare their predictive power. This revealed which type of genetic signal is more indicative of resistance. I also explored combining both feature types for improved accuracy and robustness.
