My Research

Thesis: Predicting E. coli Drug Resistance Using Traditional Machine Learning and Deep Learning

My research interests revolve around developing computational methods and machine learning and deep learning models to predict antibiotic resistance in E. coli. Currently, I am working on my thesis, using three different datasets: SNPs, Gene Presence/Absence, and Alignment-based features to predict antibiotic resistance in E. coli.

The Problem

Antibiotic resistance in E. coli is a major global health problem, as it's the most common gram-negative pathogen affecting humans. Additionally, traditional lab testing is slow and limited to cultivable strains. By applying computational models to genomic data, my goal is build generalizable models that can be used to different drugs to predict resistance.

My Solution & Methods

I am analyzing 600 samples of E. coli across three different datasets, encompassing 5.7 million nucleotide positions and 6,092 genes. I am experimenting with different machine learning models and comparing their performance to see which one is the bestfor predicting antibiotic resistance in E. coli. My goal is to build a systems that can be used to predict resistance to any drug in bacteria.

Three Datasets

  • Gene Presence/Absence: Identifies major resistance genes and virulence factors.
  • SNPs: Examines point mutations that alter susceptibility.
  • Alignment-based Features: Captures larger structural variations like insertions or deletions.

Core Research Questions

  • How does the performance of traditional models trained on pangenome datasets from two countries vary when tested on one dataset from another country?
  • Can models trained on data from multiple countries improve generalizability compared to single-country training?
  • Does combining multi-country data improve predictive accuracy?
